    # NeRF: A Volume Rendering Perspective

    # Overview

    NeRFopen in new window implicitly represents a 3D scene with a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) F:(x,d)→(c,σ)F: (\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{d}) \rightarrow (\boldsymbol{c}, \sigma)F:(x,d)→(c,σ) for some position x∈R3\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^3x∈R3, view direction d∈[0,π)×[0,2π)\boldsymbol{d} \in [0, \pi) \times [0, 2\pi)d∈[0,π)×[0,2π), color c\boldsymbol{c}c, and "opacity" σ\sigmaσ. Rendered results are spectacular.

    There have been a number of articles introducing NeRF since its publication in 2020. While most posts mention general methods, few of them elaborate on why the volume rendering procedure

    C(r)=C(z;o,d)=∫znzfT(z)σ(r(z))c(r(z),d) dz, T(z)=exp⁡(−∫znzσ(r(s)) ds) \mathbf{C}(\boldsymbol{r}) = \gray{\mathbf{C}(z; \boldsymbol{o}, \boldsymbol{d}) =} \int_{z_n}^{z_f} T\gray{(z)} \sigma \left( \boldsymbol{r}\gray{(z)} \right) \boldsymbol{c} \left(\boldsymbol{r}\gray{(z)}, \boldsymbol{d} \right) \ dz, \ T(z) = \exp \left(-\int_{z_n}^z \sigma \left(\boldsymbol{r} \gray{(s)} \right) \ ds \right) C(r)=C(z;o,d)=∫zn​zf​​T(z)σ(r(z))c(r(z),d) dz, T(z)=exp(−∫zn​z​σ(r(s)) ds)

    for a ray r=o+zd\boldsymbol{r} = \boldsymbol{o} + z\boldsymbol{d}r=o+zd works and how the equation is reduced to

    C^(r)=C^(z1,z2,…,zN;o,d)=∑i=1NTi(1−e−σiδi)ci, Ti=exp⁡(−∑j=1i−1σjδj) \hat{\mathbf{C}}(\boldsymbol{r}) = \gray{\hat{\mathbf{C}}(z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_N; \boldsymbol{o}, \boldsymbol{d}) =} \sum_{i=1}^{N} T_i \left(1 - e^{-\sigma_i \delta_i} \right) \boldsymbol{c}_i, \ T_i = \exp \left(-\sum_{j=1}^{i-1} \sigma_j \delta_j \right) C^(r)=C^(z1​,z2​,…,zN​;o,d)=i=1∑N​Ti​(1−e−σi​δi​)ci​, Ti​=exp(−j=1∑i−1​σj​δj​)

    via numerical quadrature, let alone exploring its implementation via Monte Carlo methodopen in new window.

    This post delves into the volume rendering aspect of NeRF. The equations will be derived; its implementation will be analyzed.

    # The rendering formula

    Integration bounds

    In practice, c\boldsymbol{c}c, obtained from MLP query, is a function of both position zzz (or coordinate x\boldsymbol{x}x) and view direction d\boldsymbol{d}d. Also different are the integration bounds. A computer does not support an infinite range; the lower and upper bounds of integration are znear=nearz_\text{n\gray{ear}} = \verb|near|znear​=near and zfar=farz_\text{f\gray{ar}} = \verb|far|zfar​=far within the range of floating point representation:

    C(r)=∫nearfarT(z)σ(z)c(z) dz \mathbf{C}(\boldsymbol{r}) = \int_\verb|near|^\verb|far| T(z) \sigma(z) \boldsymbol{c}(z) \ dz C(r)=∫nearfar​T(z)σ(z)c(z) dz

    In NeRF, near=\verb|near| =near= 0. and far=\verb|far| =far= 1. for scaled bounded scenes and front facing scenes after conversion to normalized device coordinates (NDC).

    # Numerical quadrature

    We took a step from discrete to continuous to derive the rendering integral. Nevertheless, integration on a continuous domain is not supported by computers. An alternative is numerical quadrature. Sample near<z1<z2<⋯<zN<far\verb|near| \lt z_1 \lt z_2 \lt \cdots \lt z_{N} \lt \verb|far|near<z1​<z2​<⋯<zN​<far along a ray, and define differences between adjacent samples as

    δi:=zi+1−zi ∀i∈{1,…,N−1} \delta_i := z_{i+1} - z_i \ \forall i \in \{1,\ldots,N-1\} δi​:=zi+1​−zi​ ∀i∈{1,…,N−1}

    then the transmittance is approximated by

    Ti:= T(zi)≈ e−∑j=1i−1σjδj \begin{align*} T_i :=&\ T(z_i) \\ \approx&\ e^{-\sum_{j=1}^{i\blue{-1}} \sigma_j \delta_j} \end{align*} Ti​:=≈​ T(zi​) e−∑j=1i−1​σj​δj​​

    where T1=1T_1 = 1T1​=1 and σj=σ(zj;o,d)\sigma_j = \sigma(z_j\gray{; \boldsymbol{o}, \boldsymbol{d}})σj​=σ(zj​;o,d). Meanwhile, differentiation in phit(z)p_\text{hit}(z)phit​(z) is also substituted by discrete difference. That is,

    Alpha compositing

    Consider the case where a foreground object is inserted ahead of the background. Now that a pixel displays a single color, we have to blend the colors of the two. Compositing is applied when there are partially transparent regions within the foreground object, or the foreground object partially covers the background.

        # NOTE: IMPORTANT!
        ###   update learning rate   ###
        decay_rate = 0.1
        decay_steps = args.lrate_decay * 1000
        new_lrate = args.lrate * (decay_rate ** (global_step / decay_steps))
        for param_group in optimizer.param_groups:
            param_group['lr'] = new_lrate
        ################################
        …

    Radiance rgb ∈RB×3\in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 3}∈RB×3 is compared against the ground truth target_s to obtain the MSE loss at line 5\verb|5|5. The total loss also includes that of the coarse network (line 12\verb|12|12). The coarse and fine network are jointly optimized at lines 15\verb|15|15 and 16\verb|16|16.
